3D printing a brain

Recently, a lab member left and I was hit by a brilliant idea: how about a giving them their own 3D printed brain as a gift? Now, if anyone had had a better idea I think it would have been likely that we'd have abandoned ship, but nobody did, and so I had to make good on my offer: 3D printing a brain, in a pandemic. This documents my efforts and the resources I found helpful.

First of all, I found three resources that were incredibly helpful:

  • This paper by Chris Madan
  • This blogplost
  • This blogpost

    • I would recommend using Chris Madan's instructions for most of it, but not bothering with the part he's written in code - the meshlab and meshgeometry and tridecimator part. Instead I would install meshlab and play with filters/remeshing, simplification and reconstruction/simplification: quadratic edge decimation, followed by filters/smoothing/laplacian.

    Make sure from meshlab you save as .stl files. You should then install Ultimaker Cura if you're printing from your computer (though not sure how it works in the Institute of Making, I think their computers all have it installed) and mess around with scaling the brain.

    The default settings will do too much detail and density for your purposes I suspect, I used layer weight of 0.2, infill density of 10% (though seen things online saying even 2 or 5% is ok), and the print temperature is material dependent but for PLA it's typically around 200 degrees.

    I would also rotate it so that the surface you're least interested in is at the bottom, and add supports and a skirt - this means that it'll print extra material so that it doesn't go badly wrong when things are not flush to the platform (turns out plastic can't stick to thin air).

    The institute of making lets you print for free if you want to use less than 100g of plastic - so that's a scale of roughly 50% on the brain (two hemispheres were each 40g or so for me).
